\chapter{Experimental Results}\label{chapt:results}

This chapter reports and discusses the experiments that evaluate the proposed
pipeline. Section~\ref{sect:res_setup} describes the experimental setup.
Section~\ref{sect:res_main} reports the main result -- \emph{Ours} versus
\textsc{Basic} -- on the default CLIP ViT-L/14 backbone, together with a
comparison against the published state of the art on the full i-CIR benchmark.
Section~\ref{sect:res_components} then dissects the pipeline on the same backbone:
it builds the method one component at a time on a cumulative ladder
(Section~\ref{subsect:res_ladder}), studies the effect of feature centering and
the remaining \textsc{Basic} post-processing
(Section~\ref{sect:res_centering}), compares the five caption-generation
instructions (Section~\ref{subsect:res_ablation_instr}), and quantifies the
benefit of embedding-space averaging
(Section~\ref{subsect:res_aug}). Section~\ref{sect:res_backbones} then varies the
backbone, showing that the ranking changes once the caption is added and
establishing the best result attainable. Section~\ref{sect:res_qual} presents a
qualitative and failure analysis, and Section~\ref{sect:res_summary} summarises
the findings.

% =====================================================================
\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sect:res_setup}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Data and protocol.} All results are reported on the full i-CIR
benchmark~\cite{psomas2025icir}: $1{,}883$ queries ranked against
$N = 752{,}092$ database images, under the instance-pool retrieval protocol of
Section~\ref{sect:method_eval}. Every number in this chapter is computed at full
scale.

\paragraph{Metrics.} Unless stated otherwise, all numbers are macro-mAP
(\textsc{mmAP}, \%), the primary metric of Section~\ref{sect:method_eval}
(Equation~\eqref{eq:macro_map}); micro-mAP (\textsc{mAP}) is reported alongside
where informative. Recall@$k$ (R@$k$, the fraction of queries with at least one
correct item in the top $k$) is additionally reported in the backbone analysis of
Section~\ref{sect:res_backbones}.

\paragraph{Configurations.} We compare the \textsc{Basic} duplet
(image$\times$text, Equation~\eqref{eq:duplet}) against \emph{Ours}, the
clamped triplet image$\times$text$\times$caption of
Equation~\eqref{eq:triplet}. The caption signal uses the \texttt{avg-5} setting
(Equation~\eqref{eq:avg5}) by default; where a single prompt is required we fix
it to instruction~\textsc{J} (\emph{draft--refine}), the best-performing
instruction on the default backbone (Section~\ref{subsect:res_ablation_instr}),
and write ``Ours~[\textsc{J}]''. Unless a backbone sweep is stated
(Section~\ref{sect:res_backbones}), all results use CLIP ViT-L/14, the default
encoder of the original benchmark. Feature centering and the \textsc{Basic}
post-processing of Section~\ref{sect:method_basic} are applied as a cumulative
ladder, analysed in Section~\ref{sect:res_components}.

% =====================================================================
\section{Main Result: Ours vs.\ \textsc{Basic}}\label{sect:res_main}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Comparison with the state of the art.}
Table~\ref{tab:res_leaderboard} compares the proposed method against the
literature on the full i-CIR benchmark, following the evaluation protocol of the
i-CIR paper~\cite{psomas2025icir}: macro-mAP on $1{,}883$ queries against the
$752{,}092$-image instance pool, CLIP ViT-L/14 backbone (the default choice in
the original benchmark). Published results (marked $\dagger\dagger$) are taken
directly from Table~1 of the i-CIR paper~\cite{psomas2025icir}; all other rows
are our own evaluation. The full result is also shown as a bar chart in
Figure~\ref{fig:res_leaderboard}.

Two ablation entries are worth noting.
\textbf{Caption alone} ($16.16$) outperforms \textbf{Caption $\times$ Image}
($14.04$), suggesting that multiplying with the image embedding can
\emph{hurt} when the backbone is weakly aligned: the image signal drags the
composed query towards unmodified instance images, diluting the textual
modification. Yet caption alone already rivals the full
\textbf{Img\,$\times$\,Txt} duplet ($17.95$), showing that a self-contained
target description is a surprisingly effective query on its own.

Our best configuration, \textbf{Ours (\texttt{avg-5})}, achieves $35.76$
macro-mAP. The strongest baseline on this benchmark is \textsc{Basic} itself: our
full reproduction \textsc{Basic}$^\dagger$ reaches $32.48$, already ahead of
every published method (the best of which, CoVR-2, scores $28.50$). Ours improves
on this strongest baseline by $+3.3$ macro-mAP, and on the best prior
\emph{published} method by $+7.3$.

\paragraph{A note on the \textsc{Basic} reproduction.}
Our \textsc{Basic}$^\dagger$ reaches $32.48$ rather than the $34.35$ reported in
the i-CIR paper~\cite{psomas2025icir}. The gap is an \emph{environment} effect,
not a code error: we re-extracted all features in a newer stack
(\texttt{open\_clip}~3.3.0, \texttt{Pillow}~12, \texttt{numpy}~2.x), and the
difference is concentrated in the image branch -- the image-only score is $2.75$
here versus $3.04$ in the paper, a $\sim\!10\%$ relative drop in the weakest
signal that is consistent with differences in image decoding and resizing across
\texttt{Pillow} versions. The text branch is unaffected, and because
\textsc{Basic}$^\dagger$ and Ours are evaluated in the \emph{same} environment,
the \emph{relative} improvement of Ours over \textsc{Basic} is independent of
this gap.

\paragraph{Headline.}
On the default backbone, adding the generated caption lifts macro-mAP from the
$17.95$ \textsc{Basic} duplet to $25.41$ at the plain product rung
(Section~\ref{subsect:res_ladder}), and to $35.76$ once the post-processing of
Section~\ref{sect:method_basic} is applied -- the best CLIP-L result and the
strongest entry in Table~\ref{tab:res_leaderboard}. The remainder of the chapter
dissects this gain component by component (Section~\ref{sect:res_components}) and
then shows how it grows on stronger backbones (Section~\ref{sect:res_backbones}).

% =====================================================================
\section{Component Analysis}\label{sect:res_components}
% =====================================================================

This section isolates the contribution of each component of the pipeline on the
default CLIP ViT-L/14 backbone, where the full \textsc{Basic} post-processing
stack of Section~\ref{sect:method_basic} -- including the CLIP-specific
projection and query expansion -- was originally designed and tuned. The
corresponding analysis on the best-performing backbone (SigLIP2) is deferred to
Section~\ref{sect:res_backbones}.

\subsection{Building the Pipeline: a Component Ladder}\label{subsect:res_ladder}

Table~\ref{tab:res_ladder} builds the pipeline up one component at a time, as a
cumulative ladder, for three configurations that share the \emph{exact same}
post-processing so the caption is the only isolated variable: \textsc{Basic}
(no caption), Ours~[\texttt{avg-5}], and Ours~[\textsc{J}] (the fixed
single-instruction variant). The first row is the plain product score; each
subsequent row adds one step of the \textsc{Basic} stack -- feature centering,
similarity min-normalisation, the Harris-style fusion penalty, query
contextualisation, the contrastive projection, and query expansion. The same
ladder is plotted in Figure~\ref{fig:res_ladder}, and the per-step deltas in
Figure~\ref{fig:res_ladder_delta}.

\textbf{The caption is a major, complementary signal.} At the plain product
level, replacing the duplet with the triplet (i.e.\ adding the \texttt{avg-5}
caption) raises macro-mAP from $17.95$ to $25.41$, a $+7.46$ gain before any
post-processing. Crucially, this gain does \emph{not} vanish once the
\textsc{Basic} stack is applied: at the contextualisation rung Ours reaches
$35.76$ against \textsc{Basic}'s $32.25$, and the best \textsc{Basic} rung
($32.48$) is still $+3.28$ below the best Ours rung ($35.76$). The caption
therefore supplies information that \textsc{Basic}'s own text contextualisation
cannot recover -- the two are complementary rather than redundant.

\textbf{Centering and contextualisation carry the post-processing.} On CLIP-L,
the largest single post-processing step is \textbf{centering}: it lifts the
\textsc{Basic} duplet by $+9.81$ ($17.95\!\to\!27.76$) and Ours by $+6.46$
($25.41\!\to\!31.87$), confirming a strong embedding-space bias.
\textbf{Contextualisation} is the next largest, adding $+3.92$ to \textsc{Basic}
and $+2.94$ to Ours. The min-normalisation and Harris steps roughly cancel. Note
that on CLIP-L the \textsc{Basic} post-processing stack (total $+14.53$ over the
product) is larger than the caption's raw $+7.46$; the picture reverses on
better-aligned backbones, where the caption dominates the entire stack
(Section~\ref{sect:res_backbones}).

\subsection{Feature Centering and Post-Processing}\label{sect:res_centering}

Reading Table~\ref{tab:res_ladder} down the rungs reveals which parts of the
\textsc{Basic} stack actually help on CLIP-L once the caption is present.
\textbf{Centering} is the single most important post-processing step: for the
\textsc{Basic} duplet it alone lifts macro-mAP from $17.95$ to $27.76$
($+9.81$), and Ours from $25.41$ to $31.87$ ($+6.46$), confirming a strong
embedding-space bias that subtracting the mean removes.
\textbf{Contextualisation} is the next clear winner, adding $+3.92$ to
\textsc{Basic} and $+2.94$ to Ours. The intermediate steps are minor:
min-normalisation slightly \emph{lowers} the score before the Harris fusion
penalty recovers it, so the two roughly cancel.

The most informative observation concerns the two final \textsc{Basic} steps,
the contrastive \textbf{projection} (Equation~\eqref{eq:basic_proj}) and
\textbf{query expansion}. The projection was designed and tuned specifically for
CLIP, and indeed it helps the \textsc{Basic} duplet here -- marginally, from
$32.25$ to $32.48$ -- but it \emph{hurts} the caption-augmented pipeline, pulling
Ours down from its $35.76$ peak to $33.80$. Query expansion then lowers every
configuration further ($-2.20$ for \textsc{Basic}, $-1.41$ for Ours). In other
words, once the caption is present even the CLIP-tuned tail of the stack becomes
counterproductive: the optimal pipeline for Ours stops at contextualisation, and
the projection and query expansion can be discarded. The same two steps are
strictly harmful on the other backbones (Section~\ref{sect:res_backbones}), so we
omit them from the default pipeline throughout.

\paragraph{Source of the centering mean.} A natural question
(Section~\ref{subsect:method_centering}) is whether the \emph{source} of the mean
matters. We find it does not: on CLIP-L, centering with the global database mean
and with a generic LAION-1M mean differ by only $\sim\!0.09$ macro-mAP, and by at
most $\sim\!0.55$ across all four backbones. The choice of centering corpus is
therefore not a material hyperparameter, and we use the global database mean
throughout.

\subsection{Caption Instruction Variants}\label{subsect:res_ablation_instr}

Table~\ref{tab:res_instr} compares the five caption-instruction variants of
Section~\ref{sect:method_caption} across three scoring modes on CLIP ViT-L/14:
caption alone ($\sc$ as a single-text query), caption $\times$ image (duplet
using $\sc$ in place of $\st$), and the full triplet (caption $\times$ text
$\times$ image, Equation~\eqref{eq:triplet}).

Three observations stand out.
\textbf{First}, caption alone consistently outperforms caption $\times$ image
for every instruction -- adding the image embedding hurts rather than helps.
This re-confirms the finding of Section~\ref{sect:res_main}: when the backbone
alignment is imperfect, multiplying with the image drags the query back towards
the unmodified instance appearance and undercuts the modification.
\textbf{Second}, caption alone already rivals the Text $\times$ Image duplet
($17.95$), showing that a well-formed target description, on its own, is nearly
as informative as combining the image and text signals.
\textbf{Third}, the triplet scores substantially outperform both caption-only
modes, confirming that the three branches are complementary: the caption supplies
the target-description signal that the modification text $\st$ cannot convey
alone, while the image branch anchors the instance identity.

The instruction ordering is consistent with design intent: \textsc{J}
(\emph{draft--refine}, $23.83$) and \textsc{G} (\emph{constraint-priority},
$23.51$) lead, while the rigid slot-fill template \textsc{I} trails. The spread
across instructions is modest ($\sim\!1.3$ points on the triplet), and all five
outperform the duplet baseline ($17.95$). Because \textsc{J} is the best single
instruction on this default backbone, we fix it as the named single-instruction
variant Ours~[\textsc{J}] throughout the thesis; \texttt{avg-5} exceeds it
(Section~\ref{subsect:res_aug}) without any per-instruction selection. The three
scoring modes are visualised in Figure~\ref{fig:res_instr}.

\subsection{Caption Augmentation: \texttt{avg-5} vs.\ Single Instructions}\label{subsect:res_aug}

Rather than commit to one prompt, the default caption embedding averages all
five instructions in embedding space (\texttt{avg-5}, Equation~\eqref{eq:avg5}).
Table~\ref{tab:res_aug} compares four caption settings on the triplet: our fixed
single instruction \textsc{J}; the per-backbone \emph{oracle} best single
instruction; an oracle average of the best four; and the unconditional
\texttt{avg-5}. The result is unambiguous: \texttt{avg-5} is best on \emph{every}
backbone. It beats not only our fixed \textsc{J} (by $+0.4$ to $+2.1$ macro-mAP)
but also the per-backbone oracle, even though that baseline is allowed to inspect
the evaluation set to pick the winning instruction. In other words, blindly
averaging all five prompts outperforms an oracle that selects the single best
one. Averaging keeps the signal that is consistent across instructions and
attenuates the idiosyncrasies of any single prompt, which is why we adopt it as
the default and reserve the fixed \textsc{J} only for analyses that need a single
prompt.

\section{Backbone Sensitivity and the Performance Ceiling}\label{sect:res_backbones}
% =====================================================================

Having dissected the pipeline on the default backbone, we now vary the encoder.
Table~\ref{tab:res_backbones} reports macro-mAP, micro-mAP and Recall@10 for both
the \textsc{Basic} duplet and Ours (\texttt{avg-5} triplet) across all four
backbones, and Figure~\ref{fig:res_backbones} shows the same comparison as a bar
chart. Two patterns emerge.

\textbf{The duplet ranking does not predict the triplet ranking.}
On the bare duplet, CLIP-H is the strongest backbone ($41.10$ mmAP), ahead of
SigLIP2 ($38.11$). Once the caption branch is added, SigLIP2 jumps to first
place ($56.09$) while CLIP-H's gain is far smaller ($47.05$). A backbone must
therefore be evaluated \emph{within} the full pipeline: a strong duplet encoder
is not necessarily the best partner for the caption.

\textbf{The triplet is uniformly better than the duplet on every backbone.}
The margin ranges from $+5.95$ (CLIP-H) to $+22.23$ (SigLIP-L). For SigLIP-L and
SigLIP2 the caption more than doubles the raw duplet score, confirming that these
encoders benefit the most from the additional textual modality. The pattern is
consistent with the caption's role: where the text encoder is poorly aligned with
the visual space, the modification text $\st$ carries little signal, and the
self-contained target caption $\sc$ supplies what is missing.

The macro-mAP (mean-per-instance AP) and micro-mAP (mean-per-query AP) rankings
agree for Ours but diverge for the duplet on SigLIP-L ($21.10$ macro vs.\
$23.13$ micro): micro-mAP up-weights queries from instances with many queries,
giving a rosier picture for that backbone. Macro-mAP is the more conservative
and instance-fair metric and is used for all headline comparisons.

Recall@10 corroborates the pattern. On the duplet, CLIP-H leads with $83.5\%$
(meaning the correct instance appears in the top-10 for $83.5\%$ of queries);
after adding the caption, SigLIP2 reaches $92.2\%$ -- a gain of $+13.3$ points --
while CLIP-H gains only $+6.4$. The recall picture (Figure~\ref{fig:res_recall})
is consistent with the mmAP ranking and further confirms that backbone choice
must be made within the full pipeline.

\subsection{Post-Processing on the Best Backbone}\label{subsect:res_siglip2_ladder}

On SigLIP2 the component analysis of Section~\ref{sect:res_components} takes its
strongest form. Table~\ref{tab:res_siglip2_ladder} repeats the cumulative ladder
on the best backbone, and Figure~\ref{fig:res_ladder_siglip2} plots it. Here the
\textbf{caption alone is larger than the entire \textsc{Basic} post-processing
stack}: at the plain product rung it raises macro-mAP from $38.12$ to $56.09$, a
$+17.97$ gain, whereas the whole \textsc{Basic} stack lifts the duplet by
$+19.57$ to its $57.69$ peak -- and the caption-augmented pipeline still leads it
by a wide margin at every rung. This is the sharpest statement of the central
empirical message: \emph{a single generated target caption injects the textual
signal more effectively than the multi-step \textsc{Basic} preprocessing it is
designed to replace.}

The remaining post-processing is complementary but secondary. Centering and
contextualisation lift Ours a further $+5.9$ to the thesis-best
$\mathbf{61.98}$ macro-mAP at the contextualisation rung. As on CLIP-L
(Section~\ref{sect:res_centering}), the two final \textsc{Basic} steps -- the
contrastive projection and query expansion -- \emph{hurt}, pulling Ours down to
$59.02$ and then $57.29$. These steps were tuned for CLIP and transfer to neither
backbone, so the optimal pipeline stops at contextualisation.

\subsection{Failure Analysis}\label{subsect:res_failure}

Examining the lowest-AP queries reveals three dominant failure modes, all rooted
in a mismatch between the requested modification and the database content.
\textbf{(i) State-change targets absent from the database.} Modifications such as
``being deflated'', ``folded'', or ``lying open'' require images of the instance
in a fundamentally different physical state. Because the i-CIR database indexes
instances in their canonical presentation, these states are absent or near-absent,
making retrieval impossible regardless of caption quality.
\textbf{(ii) Domain-transfer modifications with no database counterpart.} When
the modification requests the instance in a wholly different visual domain --
``as a painting'', ``as a film'', ``as a colorful sketch'' -- no such rendered
version exists in the database, and the retrieval is guaranteed to fail.
\textbf{(iii) Context-breaking wearable modifications.} Queries such as ``worn by
a dog'', ``worn by a person'', or ``worn on the ankle'' ask for clothing or
accessories in an atypical context. The database contains the items in isolation or
standard wear; the requested context does not appear, so AP collapses to zero.

A token-level enrichment analysis across all 1,883 queries (Appendix~\ref{app:qual_analysis})
quantifies these patterns: the token \textit{worn} (23 queries) carries a $91.3\%$
failure rate -- $3.7\times$ the global rate of $24.9\%$ -- and the bigrams
\textit{film around} and \textit{colorful sketch} cluster at $100\%$ failure.
By contrast, highly distinctive instance terms such as \textit{lego} (35 queries,
$0\%$ failures, $88.6\%$ win rate at AP$\ge$50\%) and \textit{fog} (27 queries,
$86\%$ mean AP) show that the triplet excels when the modification is concretely
expressible in language and the target images exist in the database.
Figure~\ref{fig:failure} illustrates two representative failure cases;
further panels and the domain-transfer mode are shown in
Appendix~\ref{app:qual_analysis}.

\section{Summary}\label{sect:res_summary}
% =====================================================================

The experiments support the central claim of the thesis. The generated target
caption is the single most effective component of the pipeline: it improves every
backbone (Table~\ref{tab:res_backbones}), and on the best backbone it contributes
a larger gain than the entire \textsc{Basic} post-processing stack it replaces
(Table~\ref{tab:res_siglip2_ladder}) -- all as a simple additive branch that
needs no training. On the default CLIP-L backbone the caption is complementary to
that stack, adding $+3.3$ macro-mAP even on top of the fully post-processed
\textsc{Basic} pipeline (Table~\ref{tab:res_ladder}). Embedding-space averaging
over the five instructions (\texttt{avg-5}) is a small but reliable,
selection-free improvement that beats even an oracle single-instruction baseline
(Table~\ref{tab:res_aug}). The best backbone within the full pipeline is SigLIP2,
not the strongest bare-duplet encoder (Section~\ref{sect:res_backbones}), and the
best result of the thesis -- $61.98$ macro-mAP -- combines the caption with the
two post-processing steps that transfer to SigLIP2, with the CLIP-specific
projection and query expansion discarded. Section~\ref{subsect:res_failure}
analyses the lowest-AP queries and the linguistic patterns that correlate with
failure.
