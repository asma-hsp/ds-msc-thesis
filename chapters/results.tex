\chapter{Experimental Results}\label{chapt:results}

This chapter reports and discusses the experiments.
Section~\ref{sect:res_setup} describes the experimental setup.
Section~\ref{sect:res_main} reports the main results on the full i-CIR dataset.
Section~\ref{sect:res_centering} analyses the effect of feature centering.

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sect:res_setup}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Data.} Headline results are reported on the full i-CIR
benchmark~\cite{psomas2025icir}: $1{,}883$ queries ranked against
$N = 752{,}092$ database images. Ablations and exploratory experiments use the
fixed $20\%$ instance subset (\texttt{instance\_sample20\_seed42}: $400$ queries
and $143{,}769$ database images) introduced in
Section~\ref{sect:method_impl}. The subset is a fair development proxy: the
CLIP-L duplet (text$\times$image) macro-mAP on the subset ($18.73$) closely
matches the full-dataset baseline ($17.95$), so trends established on the subset
are expected to carry over to full scale.

\paragraph{Metrics.} Unless stated otherwise, all numbers are macro-mAP
(\textsc{mmAP}, \%), the primary metric of Section~\ref{sect:method_eval};
micro-mAP (\textsc{mAP}), $\mathrm{Recall}@k$ and $\mathrm{Hit}@k$ are reported
in the comprehensive table (Table~\ref{tab:res_full}).

\paragraph{Configurations.} We compare the \textsc{Basic} duplet
(image$\times$text) against \emph{Ours} (the triplet
image$\times$text$\times$caption of Equation~\eqref{eq:triplet}), each with and
without feature centering. The caption signal uses the \texttt{avg-9} setting
(Equation~\eqref{eq:avg9}) by default; per-instruction results are reported in
Section~\ref{subsect:res_ablation_instr}. ``Ours [6\_A\_8b]'' denotes the
triplet using the single best instruction instead of \texttt{avg-9}.

% =====================================================================
\section{Main Results: \textsc{Basic} vs.\ Triplet}\label{sect:res_main}
% =====================================================================

Table~\ref{tab:res_main} reports the headline result on the full i-CIR dataset.
Adding the generated target caption improves \emph{every} backbone, with no
exception. The gains are largest for the backbones whose plain image--text
alignment is weakest: SigLIP-L improves by $+20.1$ macro-mAP and SigLIP2 by
$+16.0$, while the already-strong CLIP-H and the smaller CLIP-L improve by
$+6.7$ and $+6.5$ respectively. The best configuration in this table is SigLIP2
with the \texttt{avg-9} caption, at $54.0$ macro-mAP.

The pattern -- larger gains where the duplet baseline is weaker -- is
consistent with the role of the caption. A backbone with poorly aligned text and
image spaces produces a noisy modification-text signal $\st$; the
self-contained target caption supplies a cleaner text-side query and therefore
helps such backbones the most. Where the backbone is already well aligned
(CLIP-H), the caption still helps, but the marginal benefit is smaller because
the text branch was already informative. Comparing the two caption settings,
\texttt{avg-9} consistently outperforms the single best instruction
\texttt{6\_A\_8b} on the full dataset, confirming that embedding-space averaging
(Equation~\eqref{eq:avg9}) is a worthwhile robustness measure.

\section{Effect of Feature Centering}\label{sect:res_centering}
% =====================================================================

Feature centering (Section~\ref{subsect:method_centering}) helps every
configuration, and it composes with the caption: the two improvements are
largely complementary. Table~\ref{tab:res_centering} shows that the best result
of the thesis is obtained by combining both -- SigLIP2 with the caption and
centering reaches $59.23$ macro-mAP, up from $38.04$ for the plain duplet
baseline. Centering is especially impactful for SigLIP-L, whose uncentered
baseline ($21.10$) more than doubles with centering ($44.76$); this indicates a
strong embedding-space bias in that encoder that centering removes.

A practical question is whether the \emph{source} of the mean matters. As shown
in the bottom block of Table~\ref{tab:res_full} and summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:res_centering_mean}, centering with the global database mean and
with the LAION-1M mean differ by at most $\sim\!0.55$ macro-mAP, and usually by
less than $0.15$. The choice of centering corpus is therefore not a material
hyperparameter; we use the global database mean by default.

\section{Backbone Comparison}\label{sect:res_backbones}
% =====================================================================

Table~\ref{tab:res_backbones} ranks the backbones on the duplet
(text$\times$image) baseline on the $20\%$ subset, isolating the raw quality of
each encoder before the caption is added. On the duplet baseline CLIP-H is the
strongest backbone ($44.50$ macro-mAP@100), ahead of SigLIP2 ($33.26$), with the
smaller SigLIP-L and CLIP-L lower. The MLLM-derived Qwen3-VL-Embedding-2B is far
behind ($3.08$), confirming that an embedding model distilled from a generative
MLLM is not, by itself, competitive with contrastive dual encoders for this
retrieval task; we do not pursue it further.

It is worth contrasting this duplet ranking with the full-dataset triplet
ranking of Section~\ref{sect:res_main}, where SigLIP2 is the best backbone. The
ordering changes because the caption interacts differently with each encoder:
SigLIP2 benefits more from the added caption signal than CLIP-H does, so a
backbone that trails on the bare duplet can lead once the third modality and
centering are applied. This is precisely why the backbone must be evaluated
\emph{within} the full pipeline rather than on the duplet baseline alone.

\section{Ablation Studies}\label{sect:res_ablation}
% =====================================================================

This section isolates the contribution of each component of the pipeline on the
$20\%$ subset, where many configurations can be compared cheaply.

\subsection{Contribution of Each Component}\label{subsect:res_ablation_steps}
Table~\ref{tab:res_ablation_steps} builds the pipeline up from the duplet
baseline, one component at a time, with CLIP-L. The single largest jump comes
from the \textbf{caption}: adding it to the text$\times$image baseline raises
macro-mAP from $18.73$ to $26.49$ ($+7.8$), more than any other component.
\textbf{Centering} is the second largest contributor ($+4.8$, to $31.33$).
Crucially, once the caption and centering are present, the remaining elaborate
\textsc{Basic} preprocessing (PCA/whitening, query expansion) adds little at its
default settings ($31.71$) and only reaches $34.29$ after per-component tuning
($\lambda{=}0.2$, $N_c{=}600$, $\alpha{=}0.2$, $k{=}5$, $t{=}0.5$).

This is the core empirical message of the thesis (Framing B): \emph{a simple
target caption is a more effective, and far simpler, way to inject the textual
signal than the multi-step \textsc{Basic} preprocessing it replaces}. The
caption alone closes most of the gap to the fully tuned \textsc{Basic} pipeline,
and on stronger backbones (Section~\ref{sect:res_main}) it surpasses it.

\subsection{Caption Instruction Variants}\label{subsect:res_ablation_instr}
Table~\ref{tab:res_ablation_instr} compares the nine instruction variants of
Section~\ref{sect:method_caption} on the CLIP-L triplet. The best single
instruction is \texttt{6\_A\_8b} ($26.27$), closely followed by \texttt{1\_8b}
and \texttt{3\_8b}. The spread between the best and worst instructions
($\sim\!4$ points) shows that prompt design genuinely matters. Averaging the
embeddings of all nine (\texttt{avg-9}) lands mid-pack on the subset ($24.04$
here) but, as Section~\ref{sect:res_main} shows, is the most robust choice on the
full dataset and across backbones, which is why it is the default. In short,
the best single instruction can edge out averaging on one backbone/subset, but
averaging avoids having to pick a prompt per setting and generalises better.

\section{Reranking: A Negative Result}\label{sect:res_rerank}
% =====================================================================

We also tested whether a second, visual-only stage could refine the ranking. The
top-ranked candidates of the CLIP-L duplet retrieval were reranked by their
visual similarity to the reference image under self-supervised features from
DINOv2~\cite{oquab2024dinov2} and DINOv3~\cite{simeoni2025dinov3}. The
hypothesis was that strong instance-level visual features would promote the
correct instance within an already good shortlist.

The result is negative: reranking \emph{hurts}. Table~\ref{tab:res_rerank} shows
that reranking the duplet top-$200$ by DINO visual similarity lowers macro-mAP
from the $18.73$ baseline to $12.57$ (DINOv2) and $13.45$ (DINOv3); reranking a
shorter top-$100$ shortlist is less damaging but still does not improve over the
baseline ($16.14$ and $18.73$ respectively). Reranking by pure visual similarity
alone -- ignoring the candidate pool's text signal entirely -- is catastrophic
(macro-mAP collapses to $\sim\!3$).

The explanation is consistent with the design of the task. DINO features are
self-supervised and \emph{not text-conditioned}: they reorder the shortlist by
appearance similarity to the \emph{reference} image, which is exactly the wrong
objective at the instance level, because the correct target differs from the
reference precisely by the modification. Reranking thus pulls visual hard
negatives -- images that look like the reference but ignore the text -- towards
the top, undoing the composition that the triplet score achieved. We report this
as an honest negative result: a text-agnostic reranking stage is
counterproductive for composed retrieval, and any reranking must remain
conditioned on the modification.
