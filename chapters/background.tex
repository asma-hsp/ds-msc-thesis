This chapter lays the mathematical and architectural foundations on which the
rest of this thesis builds. It defines the core notions of content-based image
retrieval (Section~\ref{sect:bg_retrieval}), explains the Transformer
architecture and its vision counterpart (Section~\ref{sect:bg_transformer}),
reviews the dual-encoder models used for retrieval---CLIP, SigLIP, and
SigLIP2---in Section~\ref{sect:bg_clip} and Section~\ref{sect:bg_siglip}, and
describes modern multimodal large language models such as InternVL~3.5
(Section~\ref{sect:bg_mllm}). Finally, it frames composed image retrieval as a
problem (Section~\ref{sect:bg_cir}) and discusses training-free post-processing
operations that can refine retrieval without learning new parameters
(Section~\ref{sect:bg_postproc}). Detailed descriptions of the
\textsc{Basic} pipeline appear in Chapter~\ref{chapt:related}; this chapter
focuses on the underlying building blocks.

\section{Image Retrieval Fundamentals}\label{sect:bg_retrieval}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Embeddings and Similarity}

In content-based image retrieval each image is represented by a fixed-length
vector, or \emph{embedding}, produced by an encoder $\enc:\mathcal{I}\to
\mathbb{R}^{d}$. The geometry of the embedding space determines retrieval
quality: semantically similar inputs should map to nearby vectors, whereas
dissimilar ones should be far apart. The standard similarity measure is the
cosine similarity between $\ell_2$-normalised embeddings,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cosine}
  \simop(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v})
  = \frac{\mathbf{u}^{\top}\mathbf{v}}
         {\lVert\mathbf{u}\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert}.
\end{equation}
When all vectors are normalised to unit length the cosine similarity equals the
dot product, and ranking by similarity is equivalent to ranking by (negative)
squared Euclidean distance, since $\lVert u-v\rVert^{2}=2-2\,u^{\top}v$ for
unit vectors. This thesis always uses cosine similarity unless stated otherwise.

\subsection{Nearest-Neighbour Search}

Given a query embedding $q$ and a database
$\DB=\{\xdb_{1},\dots,\xdb_{N}\}$ of $N$ image embeddings, retrieval ranks the
database items by decreasing similarity $\simop(q,\xdb_{i})$. Because the i-CIR
benchmark contains approximately 752\,000 images, an exact search expressed as a
matrix--vector product is both feasible and exact; no approximate index is used.
The result of a query is a complete ranking from which all evaluation metrics are
derived.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Two elementary quantities measure retrieval quality: \emph{precision}---the
fraction of retrieved items that are relevant---and \emph{recall}---the fraction
of relevant items that are retrieved. Cutoff metrics such as $\mathrm{Recall}@k$
report the fraction of relevant items among the top $k$ results, and
$\mathrm{Hit}@k$ is $1$ when at least one relevant item appears in the top $k$
and $0$ otherwise. To summarise an entire ranking with a single order-sensitive
number, the \emph{average precision} (AP) computes the area under the
precision--recall curve. For a query with relevant set $R$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ap_basic}
  \mathrm{AP}
  = \frac{1}{|R|}\sum_{k=1}^{N}
    P(k)\cdot\mathbf{1}[\text{item at rank }k\text{ is relevant}],
\end{equation}
where $P(k)$ is the precision at cutoff $k$. The \emph{mean average precision}
(mAP) averages AP over queries. The exact trapezoidal variant used by the i-CIR
benchmark, and the distinction between macro- and micro-averaging, are defined
where they are first needed in Section~\ref{sect:method_eval}.

\section{Transformers and Vision Transformers}\label{sect:bg_transformer}
% =====================================================================

All of the encoders and language models used in this thesis are built from the
same backbone: the Transformer~\cite{vaswani2017attention}. Understanding its
core mechanism clarifies why a single architecture can serve as an image encoder,
a text encoder, and the body of a large language model alike.

\subsection{Self-Attention and the Transformer}

A Transformer operates on a sequence of token vectors. Its defining operation,
\emph{scaled dot-product attention}, lets every token aggregate information from
all other tokens with weights that depend on content. Each token is projected
into a \emph{query}, \emph{key}, and \emph{value} vector; stacking these over
the sequence gives matrices $Q$, $K$, and $V$. The attention output is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:attention}
  \operatorname{Attention}(Q,K,V)
  = \operatorname{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_{k}}}\right)V,
\end{equation}
where $d_{k}$ is the key dimension and the $\sqrt{d_{k}}$ factor stabilises the
softmax. In practice, several attention ``heads'' run in parallel on different
learned projections (\emph{multi-head attention}), letting the model attend to
different kinds of relations simultaneously. A Transformer block wraps
multi-head self-attention and a position-wise feed-forward network, each with
residual connections and layer normalisation; blocks are stacked to form deep
models. Because attention is permutation-invariant, explicit \emph{positional
encodings} are added to the token embeddings so the model can use order.

\subsection{The Vision Transformer}

The Vision Transformer (ViT)~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit} applies the Transformer
to images. An image is split into a grid of fixed-size, non-overlapping
\emph{patches} (e.g.\ $16{\times}16$ pixels); each patch is flattened and
linearly projected into a token embedding; a learned positional encoding and a
special \texttt{[CLS]} token are added; the resulting sequence is processed by a
stack of Transformer blocks. The ViT therefore has a global receptive field from
its first layer. A single image-level embedding is read out from the
\texttt{[CLS]} token or by pooling the patch tokens~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit}.
The ViT is the visual front-end of every encoder and language model introduced
below; the remainder of this chapter treats them as black boxes that map an image
or text to an embedding, differing only in how they were trained.

\section{Contrastive Vision--Language Pretraining}\label{sect:bg_clip}
% =====================================================================

CLIP (Contrastive Language--Image Pretraining)~\cite{radford2021clip} is the
canonical example of a \emph{dual-encoder} (also called \emph{dual-tower} or
\emph{two-tower}) model. A vision encoder $\encv$ (a ViT or a ResNet~\cite{he2016resnet}) maps an
image to an embedding, and a \emph{separate} text encoder $\enct$ (a
Transformer) maps a text string to an embedding in the \emph{same}
$d$-dimensional space. The encoders share no weights; matching is simply a dot
product in the shared space. This separation is what makes the architecture
ideal for retrieval: the database can be encoded once offline, while only the
query is encoded at search time.

CLIP is trained on a large collection of image--text pairs harvested from the
web (approximately 400 million pairs in the original model; open reproductions
use LAION datasets~\cite{schuhmann2022laion5b,ilharco2021openclip}). Given a
batch of $B$ pairs, CLIP's contrastive objective treats the $B$ true pairings
as positives and all $B^{2}-B$ mismatched pairings as negatives, maximising the
similarity of the former and minimising that of the
latter~\cite{radford2021clip}. With image embeddings $u_{i}$, text embeddings
$v_{j}$, and a learned temperature $\tau$, a symmetric cross-entropy
(InfoNCE)~\cite{radford2021clip} loss is applied in both directions; the
image-to-text term is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:infonce}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{i2t}}
  = -\frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B}
    \log\frac{\exp(u_{i}^{\top}v_{i}/\tau)}
             {\sum_{j=1}^{B}\exp(u_{i}^{\top}v_{j}/\tau)},
\end{equation}
and the total loss symmetrises this with the analogous text-to-image term. The
softmax in Equation~\eqref{eq:infonce} is normalised over the whole batch, so
training quality improves with batch size---a limitation addressed by SigLIP in
Section~\ref{sect:bg_siglip}.

The crucial property for this thesis is the resulting \emph{shared embedding
space}: an image and the text that describes it are mapped to nearby vectors.
This enables \emph{zero-shot cross-modal retrieval}---a text query can be matched
against image embeddings without any task-specific training. The same alignment
underlies CLIP's zero-shot transfer: to classify an image, one embeds candidate
class names as text prompts and returns the class whose text embedding is closest
to the image embedding~\cite{radford2021clip}. Because the alignment was learned
from open-domain web data, it generalises to new datasets and label sets out of
the box, which is the conceptual foundation of the zero-shot CIR setting in which
this thesis operates.

\section{Sigmoid-Loss Vision--Language Pretraining}\label{sect:bg_siglip}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{The Sigmoid Pretraining Objective}

SigLIP (Sigmoid Loss for Language--Image
Pretraining)~\cite{zhai2023siglip} preserves the dual-encoder architecture of
CLIP but replaces the softmax-based contrastive loss of
Equation~\eqref{eq:infonce} with a pairwise \emph{sigmoid} loss. Instead of
normalising over the full batch, the sigmoid loss treats each image--text pair as
an independent binary classification problem: true pairs are labelled positive
($z_{ij}=+1$) and all others negative ($z_{ij}=-1$)~\cite{zhai2023siglip},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:siglip}
  \mathcal{L}
  = -\frac{1}{B^{2}}\sum_{i=1}^{B}\sum_{j=1}^{B}
    \log\sigma\!\bigl(z_{ij}(t\,u_{i}^{\top}v_{j}+b)\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the logistic sigmoid, and $t$ and $b$ are learnable scale and
bias parameters. To compensate for the large number of negatives at
initialisation, $t$ and $b$ are initialised to $\log 10$ and $-10$
respectively~\cite{zhai2023siglip}. By eliminating the global batch-wide
normalisation, SigLIP supports memory-efficient ``chunked'' implementations that
scale to very large effective batch sizes~\cite{zhai2023siglip}. Empirically,
SigLIP performs better than the softmax loss at small batch sizes and reaches
competitive quality at moderate sizes~\cite{zhai2023siglip}, making training
more flexible than CLIP.

\subsection{Enhanced Training with Captioning and Self-Distillation}

SigLIP2~\cite{tschannen2025siglip2} extends SigLIP with several independently
developed enhancements. It adds captioning-based pretraining (using a small
decoder), self-distillation and masked-prediction losses, and a more diverse
data mixture with online curation~\cite{tschannen2025siglip2}. These additions improve multilingual
understanding, dense prediction, and localisation. SigLIP2 models outperform
SigLIP at all scales on zero-shot classification, retrieval, and representation
learning~\cite{tschannen2025siglip2}. They also support multiple input
resolutions and native aspect ratios, and are released at several sizes (86\,M
to 1\,B parameters) to trade off inference cost and
performance~\cite{tschannen2025siglip2}. Because SigLIP2 retains the same
dual-encoder architecture, existing SigLIP users can swap out the weights and
tokeniser to benefit from improved alignment and dense
features~\cite{tschannen2025siglip2}. For this thesis the relevant consequence
is empirical: SigLIP2 provides the best-aligned shared embedding space among all
backbones evaluated, as Chapter~\ref{chapt:results} shows.

\section{Multimodal Large Language Models}\label{sect:bg_mllm}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{From Language Models to Multimodal Models}

A \emph{large language model} (LLM) is a Transformer trained autoregressively
on massive text corpora to predict the next token; at scale, this yields models
that follow instructions and produce fluent, context-dependent
text~\cite{brown2020gpt3}. A \emph{multimodal large language model} (MLLM)---
also called a vision--language model in this generative sense---augments an LLM
with a visual input pathway, so it can take one or more images together with a
text prompt and generate free-form responses conditioned on both. A typical MLLM
couples a pretrained vision encoder (a ViT, Section~\ref{sect:bg_transformer})
to a pretrained LLM through a lightweight \emph{connector}---for example an MLP
projector or a set of learned query tokens---that maps visual features into the
LLM's token space; the combined model is then instruction-tuned on multimodal
data.

\subsection{InternVL-3.5}\label{sect:bg_internvl}

InternVL-3.5~\cite{wang2025internvl35} is an open-source MLLM that follows the
``ViT--MLP--LLM'' paradigm: a pretrained vision encoder processes the image and
passes features through a pixel-shuffle module and an MLP projector into the
LLM's input space, and a large language model generates text conditioned on both
the visual tokens and the text prompt. To handle high-resolution inputs, the
model uses a \emph{tiling} scheme that splits an image into several sub-tiles,
each processed independently by the vision encoder, before being concatenated
and fed to the LLM. Models in the InternVL-3.5 family are released at several
parameter scales~\cite{wang2025internvl35}.

The capability this thesis exploits is \emph{instruction-conditioned visual
description}: given the reference image and a modification text, InternVL-3.5
can be prompted to describe the imagined target image---effectively performing
the compositional edit in language space. The resulting caption is a
self-contained text query that a frozen text encoder can embed and use for
retrieval. InternVL-3.5 is used here purely as a frozen, off-the-shelf caption
generator; no fine-tuning occurs, so the overall pipeline remains zero-shot with
respect to the composed retrieval task. The choice of InternVL-3.5 over other
MLLMs is discussed in Section~\ref{sect:method_caption}.

\section{Composed Image Retrieval as a Task}\label{sect:bg_cir}
% =====================================================================

Composed image retrieval (CIR) generalises two classical retrieval modes:
\emph{content-based image retrieval} uses an image as the query, and
\emph{text-to-image retrieval} uses a text. CIR uses both modalities
simultaneously: the query is a pair $(\qv, \qt)$ of a reference image and a
modification text, and the system must retrieve the image that results from
applying the modification to the reference~\cite{vo2019tirg}. The modification
text expresses a \emph{relative} change---``the same bag but in red'', ``without
the people''---and is therefore not a stand-alone description of the target. A
CIR system must read the reference image to know \emph{what} is being modified
and the text to know \emph{how}, then combine the two.

Two design choices characterise CIR systems.

\paragraph{Early vs.\ late fusion.} \emph{Early fusion} merges the modalities
before matching, typically through a learned fusion network that produces a
single composed query embedding from $\encv(\qv)$ and $\enct(\qt)$, which is
then matched against database image embeddings~\cite{vo2019tirg}. \emph{Late
fusion} keeps the modalities separate: it computes independent similarity scores
per modality against each candidate and combines the scores, for example by a
weighted sum or a product. Late fusion avoids training a combiner, which makes
it the natural choice in the zero-shot setting; the \textsc{Basic} baseline and
the method proposed in this thesis are both late-fusion systems.

\paragraph{Supervised vs.\ zero-shot.} \emph{Supervised} CIR trains on
annotated triplets (reference, modification, target) drawn from a CIR training
set~\cite{vo2019tirg,liu2021cirr}, whereas \emph{zero-shot} CIR performs no
CIR-specific training and instead reuses frozen, pretrained
encoders~\cite{saito2023pic2word,baldrati2023circo}. This thesis operates
entirely in the zero-shot, late-fusion regime. Existing approaches that populate
these categories are reviewed in Chapter~\ref{chapt:related}.

\section{Feature Post-Processing for Retrieval}\label{sect:bg_postproc}
% =====================================================================

Even with a fixed encoder, the geometry of an embedding space can be reshaped
by simple, training-free post-processing operations. These techniques add no
learned parameters and are therefore natural components of a zero-shot pipeline.
Chapter~\ref{chapt:related} describes how \textsc{Basic} assembles them into a
complete system; here we define each operation in the abstract.

\paragraph{Centering.} Embeddings from contrastive encoders often share a
dominant common component that inflates all pairwise similarities and reduces
discriminativeness. \emph{Centering} subtracts a mean vector $\mu$ from every
embedding and re-normalises,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:centering}
  \tilde{\enc}(x)
  = \frac{\enc(x)-\mu}{\lVert\enc(x)-\mu\rVert},
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ may be estimated from the database itself or from an external
reference corpus. This removes the shared component and typically sharpens
retrieval.

\paragraph{Dimensionality reduction and whitening.} Principal component analysis
(PCA) projects embeddings onto their leading directions of variance; combining
this with \emph{whitening} equalises the variance across those directions. In
retrieval, this suppresses noisy or redundant dimensions and has a long history
as a post-processing step~\cite{radenovic2018finetuning}.

\paragraph{Query expansion.} After an initial retrieval pass, the query
embedding can be aggregated with the embeddings of its top-ranked neighbours and
the search repeated~\cite{philbin2007oxford,radenovic2018finetuning}. This
propagates information from confident matches and improves recall, particularly
in instance-level retrieval tasks.

Although these operations are conceptually simple, they can significantly improve
zero-shot retrieval quality, as later chapters demonstrate.
