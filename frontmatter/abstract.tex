Composed image retrieval searches an image database with a query that pairs a
reference image with a short modification text specifying how the desired result
should differ from it. The task is challenging: a system must interpret the
reference image, apply the requested modification, and identify the single
matching object among many near-identical distractors. The recent i-CIR
benchmark intensifies this difficulty by defining relevance at the level of a
specific object \emph{instance} rather than a general category, and by
surrounding every query with hard negatives that penalise any method relying on
a single modality. Its accompanying baseline, \textsc{Basic}, combines frozen
vision--language features with careful, training-free post-processing and proves
surprisingly competitive; it nonetheless processes the modification text in
isolation from the image it is meant to modify, and an instruction such as
``make it red'' conveys little to a text encoder that cannot determine
\emph{what} should become red.

This thesis employs language as an explicit bridge between the two modalities.
A frozen multimodal large language model reads the reference image and the
modification instruction \emph{jointly} and produces a short description of the
\emph{imagined target}, effectively performing the requested edit in language
rather than in pixels. This self-contained \emph{target caption} is embedded by
the frozen text encoder and fused with the image and text similarities through a
clamped triplet product that behaves as a soft logical \textsc{and}: a candidate
must agree with all three signals to rank highly. The pipeline requires no
training; every encoder, the caption generator, and all fusion and
post-processing steps are off-the-shelf and frozen.

The method is developed on an instance subset of i-CIR, and the successful
configurations are re-run at full scale across several vision--language
backbones. The target caption is consistently the single most effective
component: it improves every backbone, and on the strongest backbone it
contributes a larger gain than the entire \textsc{Basic} post-processing stack it
is designed to replace. On the standard CLIP ViT-L/14 backbone of the original
benchmark, the proposed method reaches $35.76$ macro-mAP, surpassing the $34.35$
reported for the published \textsc{Basic} pipeline; the best overall
configuration attains $61.98$ macro-mAP, the strongest result reported in this
thesis.
